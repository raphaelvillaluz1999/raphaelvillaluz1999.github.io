# Abstract
Shizugawa Bay in Japan’s Miyagi Prefecture is home to aquaculture farms for various seafood.
After the 2011 Tohoku Earthquake and Tsunami, the oyster raft area in the region was reduced to
one-third of the previous area, resulting in a higher oyster harvest yield. My study aims to find
the optimum oyster density for the oyster farms through incorporating oyster biological functions
into Shizugawa Bay’s hydrodynamic model system. To conduct the study field surveys
were done around Shizugawa Bay to retrieve deployed sensors collecting data on dissolved oxygen
(DO), and use other sensors for vertical profiling in the bay. The Dynamic Energy Budget
(DEB) model for bivalves, used to quantify parameters from biological processes through a representative
of used energy, was implemented into the bay’s model system as a component of the
hydrodynamic model system, then the model system was ran by configuring oyster rope density
values 0.5×, 1.0×, 1.5× and 3.0× of the oyster raft set-up in Shizugawa Bay as of February
2026 to obtain data on oyster dry weight, phytoplankton density, and dissolved inorganic nitrogen
(DIN) from 1 July 2023, 00:00 to 31 July 2023, 23:00. The model implementation was
also ran in an offline box environment for 730 days to verify initial bivalve parameters in the
COAWST run. Field survey data demonstrated the observed stratification in the bay during
the summer season, and this data suggests a struggle for phytoplankton density circulation important
for oyster growth. The COAWST runs show an experimental trend of higher depletion
intensity of phytoplankton and the occurrence of larger areas with lower phytoplankton count
as oyster density gets higher, as well as increased individual oyster dry weight as oyster rope
density was reduced. In addition the proxy total oyster meat yield in the farm is calculated
from the COAWST simulations to be highest for the 3.0× density case, and lowest for the 0.5×
density case, showing that the set-up produced dry weight per individual oyster did not compensate
for decreased oyster density. Lastly, the offline DEB model simulation returned data
consistent with expected values for oyster biological functions. In conclusion optimum farm
meat yield occurred for the 3.0× density case, but this finding sees limited use for real-world
application as environmental implications for each set-up and the mapping of economic profit
with the calculated individual oyster meat yield need further long-term study.
